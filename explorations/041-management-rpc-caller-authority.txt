Authorize management RPCs by caller identity and scoped capabilities.

Parent exploration: `022-security-vulnerability-audit.txt`

Problem:

- High-authority user, proc, and Linux management RPCs trust caller-supplied
  task, process, PID, credential, or session identifiers.
- Possession of a service send capability can therefore become confused with
  authority over the named subject.

Threat model:

- A compromised or buggy userspace server can resolve high-authority service
  ports through names or inherit them during bootstrap.
- Possession of a service send capability should not authorize arbitrary
  mutation of another task, process, Linux PID, credentials, session, or proc
  record unless the caller also holds scoped authority for that subject.

Evidence:

- `user/user/main.c` accepts credential and session mutations using
  caller-supplied task/session IDs:
  `BUNIX_USER_REGISTER_PROCESS`, `FORK_PROCESS`, `EXIT_PROCESS`,
  `SETRESUID`, `SETRESGID`, `SETGROUPS`, `APPLY_LOGIN`, `SESSION_BEGIN`,
  `SESSION_END`, and `SESSION_SET_FOREGROUND` do not authenticate that the
  sender is the Linux/proc/login authority or the subject task.
- `user/proc/main.c` accepts process-management RPCs using caller-supplied
  PIDs/task IDs.  Examples include `BUNIX_PROC_EXIT`,
  `BUNIX_PROC_SET_CMDLINE_BUFFER`, and `BUNIX_PROC_REGISTER_LINUX`, while only
  `BUNIX_PROC_SELF` visibly keys off `message.sender`.
- `user/linux/main.c` uses `message.sender` for normal Linux syscall RPCs, but
  privileged management paths such as `BUNIX_LINUX_REGISTER_PROCESS`,
  `BUNIX_LINUX_FORK_PROCESS`, `BUNIX_LINUX_EXEC_INIT`, and
  `BUNIX_LINUX_EXEC_PREPARE` use caller-supplied task/path/buffer values before
  the normal `linux_process_for()` sender check.

Root cause:

- Management protocols do not carry explicit scoped capabilities for the
  subject being mutated and do not consistently derive subject identity from
  `message.sender`.
- Read/observe service access and mutate/admin access are not separated.

Impact:

- High: a compromised server with a resolved service port may be able to alter
  credentials, sessions, proc metadata, process lifecycle, or Linux process
  registration for tasks it does not own.
- Depending on reachable capabilities, this can become privilege escalation,
  process impersonation, unauthorized process kill/exit, TTY/session confusion,
  or hiding/misreporting process state.

Exploit/reproducer plan:

- Build local server/test fixtures that use only resolved service send caps to
  attempt credential mutation, proc exit/registration, and Linux management RPCs
  for a task they do not own.
- Prove which calls currently succeed and classify each by impact before
  fixing.

Fix plan:

- Define per-service management authorities: bootstrap/proc/linux/login/user
  should hold distinct capabilities for privileged lifecycle and credential
  operations.
- Where possible, bind ordinary operations to `message.sender` and reject
  caller-supplied subject IDs that do not match the sender's authorized object.
- Split observe/read RPCs from mutate/admin RPCs so broad service resolution
  does not imply mutation authority.
- Add explicit reply/status errors for unauthorized management requests.

Regression gate:

- Focused tests proving unauthorized callers cannot mutate credentials,
  sessions, proc lifecycle, or Linux management state.
- Existing login/relogin, root/user, procfs, OpenRC, and full `make test`
  gates.
